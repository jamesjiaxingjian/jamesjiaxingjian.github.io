Chinese Indonesians

Klenteng Ancol: Keramat, lords of the land, and autochthonous relations in Chinese Batavia
======

William L. Gibson

Indonesia and the Malay World Volume 53, 2025 - Issue 157 

ABSTRACT

The Vihara Bahtera Bakti or Klenteng Ancol in north Jakarta is undoubtedly one of the oldest temples in Java, likely dating to the mid 1600s. The main deities worshipped at the temple are all endemic to Nusantara: a datuk keramat and his wife, Sam Po Kong, and deified local persons. Given the temple’s age, it offers an ideal case study for early Chinese adoption of keramat worship in Nusantara; a study of the convergence of varied religious and narratological traditions to form a syncretic mode of sanctification. While the patterns of sanctification at the temple follow patterns found elsewhere in Nusantara, there are also unique facets that merit close consideration. Notably, it may present the oldest use of the term Tua Pek Kong in the historical record. It is also one of only a handful of Taoist temples with a datuk keramat located inside the temple grounds. An examination of Tua Pek Kong and datuk keramat traditions in Jakarta and the wider region sheds light on the fusion of these modes at Ancol, which in turn informs the identities of the deified persons worshipped there today. The analysis draws comparisons with similar sites elsewhere on Java’s north coast and on Peninsular Malaysia (modern Malaysia, Singapore, and the Riau islands) to reveal complex traditions of relations with autochthonous beings known as ‘lords of the land’. This study is informed by, and in turn updates and recontextualises, previous fieldwork by scholar Claudine Salmon at Ancol and adjacent sites in the 1970s and 1980s.

ABSTRAK

Vihara Bahtera Bakti atau Klenteng Ancol di Jakarta Utara tak diragukan lagi merupakan salah satu vihara tertua di Jawa, yang kemungkinan dibangun pada pertengahan 1600-an. Dewa-dewa utama yang disembah di vihara itu semuanya endemik Nusantara: sesosok datuk keramat dan istrinya, Sam Po Kong, dan orang-orang lokal yang didewakan. Mengingat usia vihara tersebut, tempat ini menawarkan studi kasus yang ideal untuk adopsi awal pemujaan keramat di Nusantara oleh orang-orang Tionghoa di Nusantara; sebuah studi tentang konvergensi berbagai tradisi religius dan naratologis untuk membentuk suatu cara penyucian sinkretis. Sementara pola penyucian di vihara ini mengikuti pola-pola yang ditemukan di tempat-tempat lain di Nusantara, ada juga sisi-sisi unik yang layak dipertimbangkan dengan saksama. Perlu dicatat, vihara ini mungkin menampilkan penggunaan tertua istilah Tua Pek Kong dalam catatan sejarah. Vihara ini juga merupakan satu dari sedikit vihara Tao dengan sosok patung datuk keramat terdapat di dalamnya. Sebuah kajian terhadap tradisi Tua Pek Kong dan datuk keramat di Jakarta dan wilayah yang lebih luas menyoroti perpaduan cara-cara ini di Ancol, yang pada gilirannya menyingkap identitas orang-orang didewakan yang dipuja di sana saat ini. Analisis tersebut membandingkannya dengan situs-situs serupa di tempat-tempat lain di pesisir utara Pulau Jawa dan Semenanjung Malaysia (Malaysia masa kini, Singapura, dan Kepulauan Riau) untuk mengungkap tradisi kompleks yang berhubungan dengan orang-orang lokal penghuni asli tempat itu yang dikenal sebagai “tuan tanah”. Studi ini menggunakan informasi dari, dan pada gilirannya memperbarui serta merekontekstualisasi, penelitian sebelumnya yang dilakukan oleh Claudine Salmon di Ancol dan situs-situs yang berdekatan pada 1970-an dan 1980-an.

Transnational by Means of Hyperlocal: Sinophone Cinema in Singkawang (West Kalimantan)
======
Josh Stenberg
Chinese Films Abroad 2024

Abstract

Singkawang Hakka cinema represents a unique corpus in Sinophone cinema, being a small-scale industry dedicated largely to local audiences. Since 2013 these films, some of them available on YouTube, have represented a window into Singkawang’s distinctive local culture, integrating the ritual and occult sides of Hakka community life with conventional horror and romance patterns of Indonesian cinema. The cinema’s hyperlocal original and audience is a reminder of how the diversity of culture and experience is at risk of being elided in cinema in general but in Sinophone cinema in particular where incentivisation is strongly towards production in standard languages.

Chinese Indonesians Reassessed: History, Religion and Belonging
=====
Edited By Siew-Min Sai, Chang-Yau Hoon
Copyright 2013

The Chinese in Indonesia form a significant minority of about three percent of the population, and have played a disproportionately important role in the country. Given that Chinese Indonesians are not seen as indigenous to the country and are consistently defined against Indonesian nationalism, most studies on the community concentrate on examining their ambivalent position as Indonesia's perennial "internal outsider." Chinese Indonesians Reassessed argues for the need to dislodge this narrow nationalistic approach and adopt fresh perspectives which acknowledge the full complexity of ethnic relations within the country. The focus of the book extends beyond Java to explore the historical development of Chinese Indonesian communities in more peripheral areas of Indonesia, such as Medan, the Riau Islands and West Kalimantan. It reveals the diverse religious practices of Chinese Indonesians, which are by no means confined to "Chinese" religions, and celebration of "Chinese" ethnic events. Presenting a rich array of historical and contemporary case studies, the book goes beyond national stereotypes to demonstrate how Chinese Indonesians interact with different spaces and environments to establish new Chinese Indonesian identities which are complex and multi-faceted. The book engages with a larger global literature concerned with diasporic Chinese identities and practices and offers sophisticated and empirically grounded insights on the commodification of ethnic cultures and religions.
